How to verify a producer's mandate before trusting it

Updated 2026-06-19 · 8 min read
Learn the step-by-step method to confirm at the source whether an intermediary holds a current mandate from a major producer and avoid the false-mandate fraud.

How to verify a producer’s mandate (before you trust it)

In commodities, an offer almost always arrives wrapped in a claim of authority: the intermediary says they act “on behalf of” a major producer. The name cited is usually that of real, respected agribusiness companies (BRF, JBS, Marfrig, Cargill, among others) — precisely because those names inspire confidence and, for that reason, are frequently used by fraudsters to lend an appearance of legitimacy to offers. Important: citing these companies here is only an example of brands whose names are exploited by third parties; nothing on this page asserts that any specific producer took part in fraud, nor that any concrete mandate is false.

The practical point is this: the mandate — the authorization that supposedly links the intermediary to the producer — is the backbone of the operation. If the mandate does not exist, everything else (attractive prices, drafted contracts, “soft offers”, SGS reports, ICPO/FCO, MFPA/IMFPA) collapses with it. And the good news: you can verify it at the source, going straight to the producer and to official registries, without relying on any document or contact supplied by the chain of brokers.

Why verification at the source is the only reliable path

The false-mandate fraud works because it controls everything you receive: the PDF, the phone number, the “director’s” e-mail, the letterhead, even the producer’s “website”. The more documents there are, the more convincing it looks — and that is exactly the problem. Abundant documentation is not proof of a mandate; it is only proof that someone had the time to produce documents. The only way to pierce that bubble is to step out of the channel the intermediary offers and reach a source they cannot control: the producer itself, through a channel you found on your own, and the public registries of the jurisdiction. Anything that does not trace back to that independent source must be treated as unverified, no matter how polished it looks.

Below is the method in numbered steps. Each step is a concrete verification action. Do them in order, record the result of each one, and treat the operation as NOT verified until you complete all of them.

1) Identify the alleged principal and the exact claim

Write down, in one sentence, what is being asserted: which producer (principal), which intermediary (agent/mandate-holder), which product, volume, and the form of the claim (“we hold a mandate to sell on behalf of X”). Keep the original text and the documents as received, without rewriting them, and note the date and the channel through which they arrived. Precision here prevents you from “verifying” something different from what was promised — scams are often deliberately vague precisely so that any partial answer looks like a “confirmation”. Also list the proper names mentioned (alleged directors, managers, “representatives”) to check later whether they exist and actually work for the producer.

2) Find the producer’s official channel — never the contact the broker gave you

Locate, on your own, the producer’s institutional channel: Investor Relations (RI), legal/compliance, or the ethics/whistleblower channel, always starting from the company’s official website or from public registries. For publicly traded companies, the RI area is listed on the stock exchanges’ websites and in official releases; for any company, the CNPJ at the Receita points to the exact corporate name you should look for. Do not use any phone, e-mail, WhatsApp, website, or “authorization letter” supplied by the intermediary — those are precisely the elements a fraud controls. Be wary of look-alike domains (typosquatting, such as swapping a letter or using “.co” in place of “.com.br”) and of e-mails on free providers (gmail, outlook) supposedly belonging to a multinational. Check the e-mail domain character by character before writing.

3) Ask the producer directly whether that intermediary holds a current mandate

Through the official channel, ask plainly: “Does the company [intermediary], CNPJ [number], hold a current mandate to offer [product/volume] on your behalf, and is the person [name] your authorized representative?”. Ask for written confirmation, on letterhead or from a corporate-domain e-mail, and keep the reply. Phrase the question so that a generic “yes” is not enough: ask for confirmation of the scope (product, volume, validity) and of the person’s name. A lack of reply, an evasive answer, a “we do not comment on operations” that neither denies nor confirms, or a redirect back to the broker must all be treated as non-confirmation — and therefore as an unverified operation. Only an explicit “yes” from the source confirms it.

4) Check the intermediary company’s existence in the official registry

Confirm whether the intermediary legally exists in the claimed jurisdiction. In Brazil, look up the CNPJ at the Receita Federal (registration status, opening date, economic activity/CNAE, and the ownership structure). Abroad, use the corresponding commercial registry (e.g., Companies House in the United Kingdom, the state/Secretary of State registries in the US, and the equivalent commercial registries in other countries). Cross-check three points: the company exists and is active; the declared activity is consistent with international commodities trade; and the address is not merely a P.O. box or a virtual office. A newly opened company, a suspended/closed status, share capital that is trivial for the volume offered, or a CNAE incompatible with commodities trade are warning signs that reinforce treating it as unverified.

5) Examine the document and the PDF metadata

Open the file’s properties (author, generating software, creation and modification dates) and compare them with the narrative. Common inconsistencies: a file creation date later than a contract supposedly “old”; an author or company in the metadata that does not match the producer; a PDF generated by an image editor instead of corporate software; signatures that are only pasted images; logos in low resolution or stretched; contract numbers, CNPJ, or dates that do not match across pages. It is also worth running a reverse image search on the logos and searching quoted snippets of the text in search engines — fraudulent mandate-letter templates circulate and get reused. Metadata does not “prove” fraud and can be wiped by those who know how, but inconsistencies reinforce treating it as unverified until confirmation at the source; “clean” metadata also confirms nothing.

6) Verify export and sanitary authorization at the official source

For protein and food, confirm whether the plant/establishment is authorized at the official source — for example, the SIF/MAPA lists in Brazil and the establishment-authorization systems of the importing country. Check that the authorization covers exactly the product and the destination claimed: a plant may be authorized for one market and not another, or for one type of cut and not another. Also confirm that the establishment’s registration number cited in the offer corresponds, in the official list, to the producer supposedly behind the mandate. A plant not authorized for that market makes the operation unviable in practice, regardless of the mandate — and it is a check you do on your own, without relying on anyone in the chain.

7) Until confirmed, treat the operation as NOT verified

While the steps above have not returned a valid confirmation at the source, the operation remains NOT verified: do not advance any fees, commissions, “performance bonds”, “unblocking” costs, “transfer fees”, or sign exclusivity/NCNDA. In mandate frauds, a request for any upfront payment to “unlock” the operation is, in itself, an extremely serious warning sign. Urgency (“the ship sails tomorrow”), secrecy (“do not contact the producer, it’s confidential”), and “someone else is about to close” are pressure techniques designed to make you skip verification — serious verification is never in an artificial hurry, and no legitimate operation requires you to avoid speaking with the alleged principal.

What counts as valid vs. insufficient confirmation

Valid confirmation (source + independent channel): - A written reply from the producer’s official channel (RI/legal/compliance) confirming the mandate, the company, and the scope. - An official registry showing the intermediary company active and consistent with the activity. - Export/sanitary authorization confirmed at the official source for the product and destination.

Insufficient (controlled by the chain or not traceable to the source): - An “authorization letter”, contract, LOI, or “soft offer” supplied by the broker itself. - A contact (phone/e-mail/WhatsApp) given by the intermediary, without independent validation. - Screenshots, PDFs with image-only signatures, seals, or reports without verification at the issuing source. - Verbal promises, “a friend of a director”, or mandate chains (“MFPA/IMFPA”) without confirmation from the principal.

LGPD note: when collecting and processing people’s data (partners, representatives, contacts) during verification, use it only for the purpose of due diligence, on an adequate legal basis, minimize what you collect, and store it securely. Document the source of the information.

Frequently asked questions

The broker sent me an authorization letter on the producer's letterhead. Isn't that enough?
No. Any document supplied by the chain of brokers itself is insufficient, because it is precisely what a fraud controls and can reproduce. Only a reply obtained directly through the producer's official channel, located by you, counts as valid confirmation. Treat the letter as unverified until confirmation at the source.
Does citing BRF, JBS, or Cargill mean those companies are involved in a scam?
No. These names appear only as examples of major producers whose brands are frequently exploited by third parties to lend an appearance of legitimacy to offers. Nothing here accuses any producer of wrongdoing, nor asserts that any specific mandate is false. The goal is to teach the method of verification at the source.
The producer is slow to reply or does not respond through the official channel. What do I do?
A lack of reply, an evasive answer, or a redirect back to the broker must be treated as non-confirmation. The operation remains unverified and you should not advance any amounts or sign commitments. Serious verification has no artificial deadline; the haste imposed by the counterparty is, in itself, a warning sign.
Why check export authorization if the mandate looks legitimate?
Because the mandate and the authorization are independent checks. Even if there were authorization to offer, a plant not authorized for that product and destination at the official source (e.g., SIF/MAPA and the importer's system) makes the operation unviable in practice. Confirming both at the source reduces the risk of shipment and of fraud.
