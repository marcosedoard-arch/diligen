How to Avoid Commodity Scams from Brazil: A Technical Guide

Updated 2026-06-18 · 19 min read
Technical guide to avoid getting scammed buying commodities from Brazil: anatomy of the fraud, 12 red flags, the correct sequence, and a checklist before you pay.

An offer lands in your inbox or on WhatsApp. Frozen halal chicken, ICUMSA 45 sugar, non-GMO soybeans, or arabica coffee “straight from the producer in Brazil,” at a below-market price and ready to ship. The person signing isn’t the producer: it’s an “exclusive mandate,” a broker speaking on behalf of someone you’ll never meet. The tone is friendly, the documents look official, there’s a letterhead, a stamp, a registration number. And there’s urgency — the window “closes in 48 hours.”

This is the exact moment when the money in an international transaction can vanish. Not after the vessel sails, not at the port inspection: before. Before the contract is yours, before the down payment leaves your account, before any SBLC is issued. Fraud in the commodity trade rarely happens at shipment — it happens during the negotiation, in the gap between “I’m interested” and “I’ve wired the deposit.”

This guide dissects how these scams work, which signs show up early (while you can still walk away at no cost), and what the sequence of a legitimate deal looks like — so that you can tell, in minutes, a real counterparty from a well-produced performance. Authorities such as FATF/GAFI, the ICC (International Chamber of Commerce), INTERPOL, and the IC3/FBI have documented the same typologies you’ll see here for years. They repeat because they work. And they work because the buyer, rushed and afraid of losing the deal, skips the very step that would protect them: verifying the counterparty.

Worth saying up front: this text does not rely on anonymous sources, nor does it accuse any specific company or person. Every example is anonymized and describes patterns — behaviors consistent with fraud that recur across sectors and countries. Brazil is a serious exporter of protein, grains, sugar, and coffee, with thousands of legitimate transactions every day; it is precisely because the “Brazil” origin carries credibility that fraudsters use it as a front. Learning to separate the real producer from the shell that wears its name is what protects both the buyer abroad and the honest exporter here at home.

How the fraud works (the general anatomy)

Commodity scams vary in their facade — the product changes, the “origin” country changes, the company name changes — but the engineering underneath is almost always the same. Understanding the sequence is more useful than memorizing names, because the fraudster adapts the script to your sector.

The typical structure follows these moves:

  1. The bait: price and volume. The offer is too good: a price below the curve, a large volume, a short delivery window, “flexible” payment terms. The goal isn’t to be credible to a specialist — it’s to be attractive enough that you won’t want to investigate.

  2. Opaque intermediation. You never speak with the actual producer or trading house. You speak with a “mandate,” an “exclusive broker,” a “facilitator.” The distance between you and the source is the product, not an accident.

  3. Documentation for appearance’s sake. In come a SCO (Soft Corporate Offer), a proforma, “certificates,” warehouse photos, an NCNDA/IMFPA for you to sign. It all serves to create the sense that there’s a real operation behind it.

  4. The reversed order. Here is the heart of the scam. In a legitimate deal, the seller proves the goods before the buyer is exposed. In the scam, you are required to expose yourself first: sign the NCNDA, send your passport, prove your funds, pay a fee, open a banking instrument — all before any real proof that the goods exist.

  5. Extraction. The money is pulled from you through one of these vectors: an advance fee “to release,” “register,” “clear customs on,” or “activate” something; a percentage down payment on a contract that will never be fulfilled; a banking instrument issued in favor of someone with no backing; or the diversion of payment to an account that isn’t the legitimate supplier’s.

  6. Dissolution. After payment, the counterparty disappears, changes email, “has a problem at the bank,” asks for “just one more fee” to fix it, or simply stops responding. The shell company, if it ever existed, was just a husk.

The rule that sums it all up: in an honest deal, the party holding the goods takes the first risk of proof; in a scam, the party holding the money takes the first risk of loss. Whenever someone asks you to expose yourself before the seller does, stop.

Notice that nothing in this sequence depends on the fraudster being sophisticated. They don’t need to hack anything or forge a perfect document — they only need a victim in a hurry, afraid of losing a good offer, and willing to trust appearances over verification. That’s why the most effective defense isn’t technological: it’s procedural. It’s having a fixed order of steps (the choreography we detail later) and refusing to move forward when someone tries to break it.

The most common types

What follows are the six typologies that show up most often in the Brazil–world commodity trade. Each has a “how it works” and the “signs” that give it away early.

Fake producer mandate

How it works. Someone presents themselves as the exclusive representative, agent, or “mandate” of a large, well-known Brazilian producer or meatpacker. They use the company’s real name, sometimes even its logo and plant photos lifted from the official website. The offer goes out under that borrowed reputation. The fake mandate is powerful because it transfers a legitimate brand’s credibility to the fraudster — you let your guard down because “everyone knows” the producer being cited.

Signs. The supposed mandate never connects you directly to the producer; all communication runs through them. The email is from a free domain or from a lookalike domain resembling the producer’s, not the real corporate domain. The power of attorney, where one exists, can’t be confirmed through the producer’s official channel. And when you call the meatpacker’s main line and ask for the representative by name, no one recognizes them.

Advance-fee (upfront fees)

How it works. The contract advances to a point where, suddenly, a fee appears that you must pay before receiving anything: an “export registration fee,” a “customs clearance fee,” a “performance bond,” an “advance SGS inspection fee,” a “POP issuance cost.” The amount is usually small relative to the contract — which is deliberate, so it looks like a bureaucratic detail. Pay one, and another appears. The advance-fee structure lives on stacking charges, each one “the last,” until the buyer gives up or runs out of money.

Signs. Any payment request that precedes the delivery of verifiable value. Fees with official-sounding names that don’t match how that cost actually works (in legitimate exporting, several of those costs are the seller’s or are already built into the price). A change in amount or account at each step. Urgency tied to paying the fee (“if you don’t pay today, you lose the lot”). A useful mental test: ask “if this were real, would this cost be mine, and would it be paid now, in this way?” In nearly every advance-fee scheme the honest answer is no — the cost wouldn’t exist, or it wouldn’t be yours, or it wouldn’t be paid upfront.

SBLC leasing/monetization

How it works. Here the “product” stops being the chicken or the sugar and becomes the financial instrument. They offer “SBLC leasing,” “BG/SBLC monetization,” “MT760 activation” — promises that a banking instrument (a Standby Letter of Credit) will be issued or leased to unlock the deal, in exchange for an upfront fee. This is a classic finance fraud that infiltrates the commodity trade because the two worlds share the same vocabulary (SBLC, MT103, MT760, POP).

Signs. The very phrase “SBLC leasing” is already a warning: serious banks don’t “lease” guarantees to third parties without backing. A request for an upfront fee to “issue,” “activate,” or “transmit the MT760.” A promise of a disproportionate financial return. Language that confusingly mixes banking instruments with the sale of the goods, blurring two operations that should be kept separate.

Broker chains / abusive non-circumvention

How it works. Between you and the supposed source sits a line of intermediaries — the broker of the broker of the mandate — each demanding that you sign an NCNDA/IMFPA and invoking non-circumvention clauses (“don’t go around me”) to justify why you can never speak with the producer. The non-circumvention clause legitimately exists to protect the commissions of whoever brings a deal. In the scam, it’s used as a curtain: it serves to block verification, not to protect a real commission.

Signs. An excessive number of intermediaries with no clear function. A refusal to reveal the source “because of the NCNDA.” Demands to sign confidentiality and commission agreements before any proof of product. Each link points to the next, and the real producer never appears at the end of the chain. Pressure not to “skip steps” by going straight to the source — the exact opposite of what healthy due diligence requires.

Payment diversion

How it works. This is the typology that attacks real deals, including those between legitimate parties. A criminal compromises an email account (the supplier’s, the buyer’s, or an intermediary’s) and, at the moment of payment, sends new “updated” banking details: “we changed banks,” “the old account is under audit,” “use this branch.” The money goes to the fraudster’s account. Payment diversion (also called BEC, Business Email Compromise) is one of the frauds widely documented by the IC3/FBI precisely because the deal itself is real — only the destination of the money has been hijacked.

Signs. A last-minute change of banking details, especially near payment. An account in a country or under a name that doesn’t match the supplier’s. Small variations in the email address (a swapped letter, a .co domain instead of .com). Urgency and an emotional justification for the change. A refusal to confirm the new details through a second channel (a known phone number, not the number that came in the same email).

Forged documents (authenticity)

How it works. To support any of the frauds above comes the documentary apparatus: an SCO, a proforma, a “certificate of origin,” an “SGS quality certificate,” an export license, a bank statement showing funds, even a “bank guarantee.” Today, editing a PDF, cloning a letterhead, or generating a perfect-looking document is trivial. The forged document doesn’t need to fool an expert — it needs to fool you, at the end of a busy day.

Signs. Documents that don’t survive a metadata examination (a creation date later than the printed date, an inconsistent author/software, recent edits). Certificate numbers that don’t appear in the issuer’s database. Low-resolution logos, swapped fonts, identical stamps on supposedly independent documents. A refusal to provide the document verifiably and directly at the source (e.g., the inspection company issues or confirms the certificate through its own channel, not through a PDF the seller sends you).

A practical detail: OSINT techniques (open-source intelligence) resolve much of this at no cost. An inspection certificate is confirmed on the inspection company’s website or help desk; a corporate registration is cross-checked against the country’s official registry; a domain is verified by its creation date and history; an “exclusive” warehouse photo often turns up in a reverse image search as a stock photo or another company’s image. The fraudster is betting you won’t spend those ten minutes. Spend them — or ask someone who does this for a living.

The 12 red flags (explained)

Each red flag below is, on its own, reason to slow down. Two or more together: treat it as NO-GO until there is robust proof to the contrary.

  1. Reversed order. You were asked to prove funds, sign instruments, or pay before any real proof that the goods exist. In a healthy deal, the seller proves the product first. Reversed? Stop and reset the order — anyone who won’t reverse it back is selling risk, not commodity.

  2. Passport and sensitive data early. Right at the start they ask for a copy of your passport, detailed proof of funds, or complete banking details “for the NCNDA.” Provide the minimum, and late; sensitive data early feeds both identity fraud and the scam’s theater of legitimacy. Remember the LGPD: you are also accountable for what you collect and share.

  3. The 48-hour pressure. “The offer expires today,” “another buyer is closing,” “the lot ships tomorrow.” Artificial urgency exists to suppress verification. A real commodity doesn’t evaporate because you asked for 48 hours to check. Whoever sets the clock is whoever profits from you not checking.

  4. A mandate with no validation at the source. They present themselves as a producer’s representative, but you can’t confirm it through the producer’s official channel. Call the main institutional line, write to the real corporate domain, and ask whether that name represents the company. Without confirmation at the source, the mandate is worth nothing.

  5. A document that doesn’t survive examination. Before trusting any PDF, look at the metadata (the file properties) and confirm certificate numbers directly with the issuer. If the creation date, the author, or the edit history don’t match what the document claims, treat it as forged until proven otherwise.

  6. A company/LLC that doesn’t exist in the registry. The counterparty cites a company that doesn’t appear in the public records of the stated country, or appears as having been opened a few weeks ago, with no history. Check the CNPJ in Brazil, or the equivalent registry (Companies House, a state LLC registry, etc.) abroad. A freshly created or nonexistent shell is a strong indicator.

  7. Incompatible economic activity. The company exists in the registry, but its corporate purpose or its CNAE code has no connection to exporting the commodity in question — a “consultancy” selling 10,000 tonnes of sugar, for example. A corporate structure that doesn’t match the operation offered is a pattern consistent with a front.

  8. Thematic incongruity / wrong certification. The offer mixes certifications that don’t apply to the product: a “halal” seal on a cargo where it makes no sense, an “ICUMSA certificate” described in a technically incorrect way, a nonexistent soybean grade. A technical error from someone who should know the product is a sign that the counterparty doesn’t actually operate in that market.

  9. Payment to a third party. They ask you to pay into an individual’s account, a company under a different name, or a jurisdiction unrelated to the seller or the goods. Legitimate payment goes to the supplier’s corporate account, in its name, consistent with the operation. A third-party account = freeze everything.

  10. Refusal to prove the product. You ask for verifiable proof — an independent inspection, a POP (Proof of Product) checkable at the source, a video with the day’s code, a warehouse visit — and you get evasions, more paper documents, or “that only happens after the NCNDA and the deposit.” A real seller wants to prove they have the cargo; a fraudster wants to defer the proof until you’ve paid.

  11. Switching intermediaries without formalizing. Mid-negotiation, the contact changes, the company that “now handles this” changes, the bank changes — with no document, no formal explanation. Each informal switch is an opportunity for diversion (see payment diversion). Demand formalization and confirm through a second channel before accepting any change.

  12. Advance-fee / SBLC structure. At some point, the deal depends on you paying an upfront fee “to release” something, or it revolves around “SBLC leasing/monetization.” These two structures are a recurring pattern in documented fraud. Found it? The presumption should be NO-GO until proven otherwise with real backing.

The CORRECT sequence of a legitimate transaction

The best defense isn’t diffuse suspicion — it’s knowing the right choreography. When you know how a real deal flows, the scam’s performance becomes obvious, because it always breaks the order. The sequence below is the pattern recognized in international commodity trade; there are legitimate variations, but the logic of who takes risk first holds.

  1. LOI / ICPO with real identification. The buyer expresses interest via an LOI (Letter of Intent) or, depending on the custom of the deal, an ICPO (Irrevocable Corporate Purchase Order) — with the real, verifiable identification of the parties, not an anonymous figure behind a broker. Right here, buyer and seller are checkable entities.

  2. Verifiable proof of product. Before the buyer is financially exposed, the seller demonstrates that they have the goods in a way that is checkable at the source: an independent third-party inspection (SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek — contracted or confirmed directly with the inspection company), a POP that can be validated, a dossier that survives examination. Proof of product comes before any down payment — never the other way around.

  3. SPA — the real contract. The parties negotiate and sign the SPA (Sales and Purchase Agreement), the detailed contract: specification, Incoterms, shipment schedule, inspection, payment terms, governing law, and forum/arbitration. It is the SPA that governs the deal — not the initial SCO, nor WhatsApp promises.

  4. Reciprocal KYC. Verification runs both ways. You perform KYC/due diligence on the counterparty, and it is legitimate for them to do the same on you. Corporate documents, proof of existence and activity, ultimate beneficial owners, sanctions (AML). Reciprocity is a good sign; pressure for only you to be exposed is a bad sign.

  5. Banking instruments in the right order. Only then do the instruments enter — a documentary LC (Letter of Credit), an SBLC, MT103/MT760 — operated bank-to-bank, against documents, in the sequence set out by the SPA. The banks and the terms of the documentary credit protect both sides; payment is tied to the presentation of conforming shipping documents, not to blind trust.

The choreography in short: real identification -> proof of product -> contract (SPA) -> reciprocal KYC -> banking instruments against documents. Every time someone asks for money, a fee, or an instrument before proof of product and the SPA, the order has been broken on purpose.

Checklist before you sign or pay

Use this as a gatekeeper. If you can’t honestly check off most of these, don’t move forward.

  • [ ] I identified the real counterparty (not just the broker), and it exists in the public registry of the stated country, with a history and an activity consistent with the operation.
  • [ ] I confirmed the mandate at the source, through the producer’s/trading house’s official channel — the institutional phone line and corporate domain, not the contact the intermediary gave me.
  • [ ] I demanded and obtained verifiable proof of product (an independent inspection confirmed at the source, a checkable POP) before any payment or instrument.
  • [ ] I examined the documents beyond appearances: metadata, certificate numbers confirmed with the issuer, technical coherence of the product and the certifications.
  • [ ] I paid no upfront fee to “release,” “register,” “activate,” or “monetize” anything. No advance fee. No SBLC leasing.
  • [ ] The payment account belongs to the supplier, is corporate, in its name, in a coherent jurisdiction — and any change of details was confirmed through a second, independent channel.
  • [ ] I have an SPA governing the deal, with Incoterms, inspection, payment terms, and forum/arbitration defined.
  • [ ] The banking instruments enter in the right order, bank to bank, against documents — and my bank/trade finance team reviewed the terms.
  • [ ] There is no artificial urgency pushing me to skip any item above.
  • [ ] I documented everything: emails, domains, documents, and identities, for an evidence annex in case I need to escalate.

When to engage counterparty due diligence

Verifying a counterparty on the other side of the world, in another language, in another legal registry, under deadline pressure, isn’t something you do well off the cuff between one email and the next. You can check a CNPJ or a Companies House record on your own; it gets much harder to cross-reference ultimate beneficial owners, validate a mandate directly with the producer, examine the metadata of a dozen documents, trace lookalike domains, and build a defensible verdict before the deadline closes.

FAQ

I received a commodity offer well below market price. Is a low price alone already a scam?

A price below the curve doesn’t prove fraud, but it’s the classic bait. Treat it as a signal to increase verification, not to speed up. Confirm the counterparty at the source, demand verifiable proof of product before any payment, and be wary if the bargain comes paired with urgency and a reversed order (you expose yourself first).

The seller insists I sign an NCNDA and send my passport before showing me any proof of product. Is that normal?

Not in the order they’re asking. An NCNDA and identification data have a legitimate place in a deal, but late and on a reciprocal basis. Demanding that only you expose yourself (passport, funds, signatures) before the seller proves they have the goods is a pattern consistent with a scam. Reverse the order back; whoever refuses is selling risk.

How do I verify whether a mandate really represents the Brazilian producer it cites?

Go to the source, not the intermediary. Look up the producer’s/meatpacker’s official institutional phone line and corporate domain and ask whether that person represents them. Be wary of emails from free domains or lookalike domains resembling the official one. A mandate that doesn’t survive a call to the source is worth nothing.

What is SBLC leasing or monetization, and why is it cited as a red flag?

These are schemes that promise to ‘lease’ or ‘activate’ a banking instrument (SBLC/BG) in exchange for an upfront fee, usually with a disproportionate return. Serious banks don’t lease guarantees to third parties without backing. The presence of ‘SBLC leasing’ or a request for a fee to ‘issue the MT760’ is a recurring pattern in documented fraud and should be treated as NO-GO until there is robust proof to the contrary.

On payment day, the supplier sent new banking details saying they changed banks. Can I pay?

Stop. A last-minute change of banking details is the signature of payment diversion (BEC), which attacks even real deals via a compromised email account. Confirm the new details through a second, independent channel (a phone number you already know, never the number that came in the same email) and be wary of an account whose name or jurisdiction doesn’t match the supplier.
