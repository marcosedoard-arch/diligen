Free self-test vs. Raio-X de Contraparte: which to use

Updated 2026-06-19 · 5 min read
Free self-test (12 red flags, indicative) versus professional Raio-X (official sources, OSINT, evidence, GO/NO-GO/EDD verdict). When to use each.

Before closing a commodities deal with a counterparty you barely know, there are two ways to reduce the risk of falling for fraud: run a free self-test on your own, or commission a professional Raio-X de Contraparte. Both are useful. But they serve different moments, and confusing one for the other can be costly — whether by spending where you didn’t need to, or by saving where you shouldn’t have.

This article explains, without inflating anything, what each one really delivers, where the self-test is enough and where you need to escalate.

What the free self-test is

The self-test is an indicative screening tool that you answer yourself. It’s 12 questions — the 12 most classic red flags of commodities fraud — designed so that anyone, even without diligence experience, can spot warning signs in a few minutes.

It covers things like: did the counterparty ask for an advance fee (“advance fee”)? Does contact happen only through informal channels? Is there urgency pressure to sign? Did the documents arrive as low-quality images or PDFs with no verifiable origin? Does the chain have too many brokers and no clear owner of the goods? Is the “proof of product” generic or unverifiable?

Characteristics of the self-test:

  • Free and immediate.
  • 12 standardized red flags.
  • Indicative — it points to a probability of risk, it doesn’t prove anything.
  • You answer it yourself — it depends on the information you already have and on your honesty when answering.

The value of the self-test is enormous for what it sets out to do: it stops you from advancing into a deal that was already screaming “fraud” from the first email. Many people lose money to scams that the self-test would have flagged in five minutes.

What the professional Raio-X de Contraparte is

The Raio-X is an independent investigation, conducted by a third party, that doesn’t depend on what the counterparty says nor on what you think. Instead of “you answering”, Diligencir goes out to find the answers in official and open sources, and delivers evidence.

What the Raio-X does that the self-test does not:

  • Independent verification in official sources: corporate records, registration status, lawsuits, regulatory status, and confirmation of existence and real capacity.
  • OSINT (Open-Source Intelligence): media, history, sector reputation, links.
  • Asset-graph: mapping of hidden partners, shell companies and structures used to conceal the ultimate beneficiary or to launder a reputation.
  • Evidence with hash/timestamp: each finding comes documented and dated, with verifiable integrity — something worth its weight in gold if the deal ends up in dispute.
  • GO / NO-GO / EDD verdict: a clear recommendation. GO (you may proceed), NO-GO (don’t close), or EDD (Enhanced Due Diligence — there are points that require deeper investigation before deciding).

It comes in three formats: Express (48h) for a quick check before moving ahead, Completo (5-7 days) for an in-depth investigation before a significant operation, and Sentinela for ongoing monitoring of recurring counterparties.

Who each one is for

The self-test is for anyone on the front line: the salesperson who received an offer, the broker assessing whether it’s worth pursuing, the business owner doing a first read. Zero cost, no barrier to entry, ideal for filtering out the obvious before spending time or money.

The professional Raio-X is for whoever is close to committing capital, goods or reputation. When the check is significant, when the counterparty is new, when the deal is heading to a contract — that’s where guesswork gets expensive and you need independent evidence, not a self-assessment.

When the self-test is enough and when to escalate

The self-test is enough when:

  • You’re at a very early stage, just screening whether it’s worth a conversation.
  • The value at stake is low and the relationship is long-standing and already known.
  • You just want a quick sanity check before asking the counterparty for more information.

Escalate to the Raio-X when:

  • The self-test lit up one or more red flags — any serious sign calls for independent verification.
  • The value of the operation is significant enough that a fraud would hurt.
  • The counterparty is new, foreign, or came through a third-party referral you don’t control.
  • You’re going to sign a contract, open a letter of credit or make any payment.
  • You need timestamped evidence to protect yourself in a possible dispute or audit.

The practical rule is simple: the self-test filters, the Raio-X decides. Use the self-test to avoid wasting time on what is clearly bad. Use the Raio-X to have well-founded courage (or caution) when the deal is serious. And importantly: passing the self-test is not a GO. The absence of obvious red flags doesn’t prove underlying capacity or mandate — it just means that the scam, if there is one, is more sophisticated than the 12 questions can catch on their own.

Conclusion

The free self-test is the honest entry point: fast, useful and sufficient for much of everyday screening. The Raio-X de Contraparte is what you commission when the “maybe” needs to become GO, NO-GO or EDD with evidence behind it. Start with the self-test whenever you have a doubt. Escalate to the Raio-X whenever the answer really matters to your cash.

Frequently asked questions

If I pass the self-test with no red flags, can I close the deal safely?
Not necessarily. The self-test is indicative and covers 12 classic fraud signs that you answer yourself — the absence of red flags means nothing obvious showed up, not that the underlying capacity, the mandate and the ability to deliver were verified. For significant operations, escalate to the Raio-X, which confirms that information independently.
Why pay for the Raio-X if the self-test is free?
Because they do different things. The self-test depends on what you already know and only flags risk. The Raio-X seeks evidence in official sources and OSINT, maps hidden partners (asset-graph), verifies documents and mandate independently and delivers everything with hash/timestamp and a GO/NO-GO/EDD verdict. When the value at stake is high, that evidence pays for its cost.
Which version of the Raio-X should I choose: Express, Completo or Sentinela?
Use Express (48h) when you need a quick check before advancing a negotiation. Use Completo (5-7 days) before signing a contract or paying in a significant operation, with an in-depth investigation. Use Sentinela when the counterparty is recurring and you want ongoing monitoring, so you're alerted if something changes after the first verdict.
