Counterparty due diligence vs. your bank's KYC

Updated 2026-06-19 · 5 min read
Bank KYC verifies identity and AML to open an account; it doesn't investigate underlying capacity, mandate, OSINT or commodities fraud.

When a Brazilian company closes a deal with a commodities counterparty abroad — or receives a proposal from overseas — almost the same question always comes up: “But doesn’t the bank already check this? If they approved the account, shouldn’t the company be trustworthy?”

The short answer is: not the way you imagine. The KYC (“Know Your Customer”) that the bank performs and the counterparty due diligence that protects your operation answer different questions, for different clients, at different depths. They complement each other — neither replaces the other. This article explains, without beating around the bush, what each one covers, what it doesn’t, and how to use both.

What your bank’s KYC actually does

Bank KYC exists for a regulatory reason: the financial institution needs to know who the account holder is in order to comply with anti-money-laundering rules (“AML” — Anti-Money Laundering) and counter-terrorism financing rules. The bank’s goal is to protect the bank itself and the financial system, not to protect your purchase-and-sale contract.

In practice, a KYC process typically verifies:

  • Identity of the holder and of the ultimate beneficial owners (“UBO” — Ultimate Beneficial Owner).
  • Corporate documents and basic registration status.
  • Sanctions lists (OFAC, UN, EU), “PEP” (Politically Exposed Person) lists and restrictive lists.
  • Declared source of funds and expected transaction profile.
  • Transaction monitoring to detect atypical money-laundering patterns.

This is essential and well done. But note the scope: the bank wants to know whether it can open and maintain an account for that entity without incurring regulatory risk. It is not asking whether that counterparty will deliver 25,000 tonnes of VHP sugar at the agreed port, at the agreed price, with the underlying capacity it claims to have.

What your bank’s KYC does NOT do

Here is the gap that catches many people off guard. Bank KYC typically does not investigate:

  • Operational capacity (underlying assets): does the counterparty actually have the product, the mill, the farm, the warehouse, the supply contract? Or is it an intermediary with no ability to deliver?
  • Authenticity of mandate: does the person signing the “LOI”, the “ICPO” or the contract have the authority to do so? Are the mandate, the power of attorney and the “corporate authority” genuine?
  • OSINT (Open-Source Intelligence): what do open sources, media, court records, litigation history and sector reputation reveal about that counterparty and its controllers?
  • Asset-graph: how is the company connected to other entities, hidden partners, shell companies and structures used to conceal the real beneficiary or to launder a burned reputation?
  • Commodities fraud patterns: the classic frauds of the sector — fake “SBLC”/”BG” documents, fabricated “proof of product”, advance fees (“advance fee”), chains of brokers who never touch the goods, the “soft probe / hard probe” scam.

The bank’s KYC confirms that the counterparty exists and is not sanctioned. It does not confirm that the counterparty will perform the deal nor that the offer is real. That is the difference that costs money.

Who each one is for

The bank’s KYC is for the bank. You don’t choose to hire it; it happens because regulation requires it. You benefit indirectly — the system gets cleaner — but the result doesn’t reach you in a form you can act on for your commercial decision.

Counterparty due diligence is for whoever is going to sign, pay or ship. Trading companies, exporters, importers, mills, cooperatives, brokers who want to protect their own reputation, and any company about to commit capital or goods to a counterparty it barely knows. When to use both (and why they’re complementary)

They don’t compete — they operate at different layers of risk:

  1. The bank does KYC to let the entity move through the financial system. This filters out the obvious: sanctioned parties, restrictive lists, nonexistent identities.
  2. You do counterparty due diligence to decide whether that specific deal, with that specific counterparty, under those conditions, makes sense. This filters out what the bank never looked at: underlying capacity, mandate, sector fraud, hidden links.

Think of it this way: KYC answers “can this company have an account?”. Due diligence answers “should I entrust 2 million dollars of goods to this company?”. They are different questions. Passing the first does not answer the second.

The expensive mistake is treating bank approval as a seal of commercial trustworthiness. Commodities fraudsters frequently do have legitimate bank accounts — opening an account is easy; delivering the product is the hard part. KYC was not designed to catch this kind of risk, and that’s fine: it isn’t its job. The job belongs to your diligence.

Conclusion

Bank KYC and counterparty due diligence are partners, not competitors. One ensures the counterparty can operate in the system; the other ensures that you should operate with it. Trust the bank for what it does — and don’t outsource to the bank a decision that is yours. Before signing or paying, ask for the verdict that matters to your cash: GO, NO-GO or EDD.

Frequently asked questions

If the bank has already approved the counterparty's account, do I still need due diligence?
Yes. Bank approval means the entity passed KYC/AML and can operate in the financial system — not that it has the underlying capacity to deliver the goods nor that the offer is real. Commodities fraudsters often have legitimate accounts. Counterparty due diligence verifies operational capacity, authenticity of mandate and fraud patterns that KYC does not cover.
What's the practical difference between checking sanctions and doing counterparty due diligence?
Checking sanctions and lists (OFAC, UN, PEP) is one item within a much larger scope. Counterparty due diligence includes that check, but adds OSINT, asset-graph (links and hidden partners), independent verification of documents and mandate, and analysis of the commodities sector's fraud patterns — ending in a GO/NO-GO/EDD verdict.
Does the Raio-X de Contraparte replace the bank's KYC?
No, and it shouldn't. They are complementary layers: KYC is mandatory and protects the bank and the financial system; the Raio-X protects your specific commercial operation. Use KYC for what it does, and the Raio-X to decide, with timestamped evidence, whether to close, renegotiate or refuse that deal.
