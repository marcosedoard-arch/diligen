Due diligence in chicken exports from Brazil to China

Updated 2026-06-19 · 6 min read
How to verify a frozen chicken deal between Brazil and China: GACC registration, SIF/MAPA plant, fake mandates, and the red flags that expose the front.

China is the largest buyer of Brazilian chicken, and legitimate operations move containers of frozen cuts, chicken paw, and giblets every single day. That is precisely why the Brazil->China route attracts fraudsters: the name of a major Brazilian poultry processor and the credibility of the origin become a front for offers that never ship. This article shows what the real operation looks like, which scams are typical of this route, and how to verify the counterparty before signing or paying.

As our pillar guide to anti-fraud due diligence in commodities lays out, the golden rule applies here too: whoever holds the goods bears the first burden of proof; whoever holds the money should not bear the first risk of loss.

What the legitimate operation looks like

Exporting chicken from Brazil to China is not something any company can do. There is a chain of sanitary and regulatory accreditation that separates those who actually export from those who merely pretend to:

  • A plant with SIF (Servico de Inspecao Federal) from MAPA. The slaughterhouse/processor must be registered with the SIF and under the oversight of the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock (MAPA). Without SIF, the product has no federal inspection and cannot be exported.
  • GACC accreditation (General Administration of Customs of China). China requires every exporting plant to be accredited and officially listed by the GACC. Each establishment has a Chinese registration number. A plant that is not accredited simply cannot clear cargo at destination.
  • Halal certification, where applicable to the market, and sanitary documentation (international sanitary certificate) issued by the competent authority.
  • Cold-chain logistics: reefer containers, a coherent BL (Bill of Lading), third-party inspection (for example, SGS) when contracted.

In practice, the number of Brazilian plants accredited to export poultry to China is limited and publicly verifiable. They are large groups, with a name, a CNPJ, and a track record. That is the key point: the legitimate operation is traceable back to a listed plant.

Why the “tiny foreign trading company” is usually implausible

The most common scam on this route arrives like this: a small “trading company,” based in a jurisdiction with no connection to poultry (a recently formed LLC in the US, a Hong Kong company with no track record, a Middle Eastern intermediary), offers large volumes of Brazilian frozen chicken at below-market prices, with fast delivery.

Stop and think about the real chain. The chicken leaves a SIF plant accredited by the GACC. Those plants belong to groups that already have structured sales channels into China. Why would such a lot leave through an unknown micro-trading company with no structure, instead of the export group’s own official channels? In most cases the honest answer is: it would not. The interposition of a tiny trading company between you and an accredited plant is, in itself, a sign of incongruence between the structure being offered and the real operation.

The scams typical of this route

Fake mandate of a major exporter

The central typology. Someone presents themselves as the exclusive mandate or representative of a well-known major Brazilian poultry processor, using the real name, the logo, and even photos of the plant lifted from the official website. The fake mandate scam works because it transfers the credibility of the famous brand to the fraudster: you lower your guard because “everyone knows” the group being cited. The mandate letter may be entirely forged, or simply never issued by the company named.

The decisive sign: all communication runs through the intermediary, and you are never connected directly to the processor through its official channel.

Invented or outdated GACC/SIF accreditation

The fraudster cites a GACC registration number or a SIF that looks official but that (a) does not exist on the list, (b) belongs to another plant, or (c) is suspended/de-accredited. Because most buyers do not know these lists are public and verifiable, the front holds up.

Advance-fee disguised as a regulatory cost

An upfront fee appears to “register the export,” “accredit the lot with the GACC,” “release the sanitary certificate,” or “advance the SGS inspection.” The advance-fee structure lives on stacking charges: you pay one, another appears. In legitimate poultry exports, those costs belong to the seller or are already built into the CIF price - they are not fees the buyer advances separately.

Payment diversion

In real operations, close to payment an email “from the supplier” appears with new banking details (“we changed banks,” “the old account is under audit”). The money goes to the fraudster’s account.

Red flags specific to the Brazil->China chicken route

  1. A seller that is not a SIF plant accredited by the GACC - and cannot point to which listed plant the product ships from.
  2. A foreign micro-trading company intermediating a large volume of Brazilian chicken with no structure or track record in the poultry sector.
  3. A GACC or SIF number that does not match the official list, or that belongs to another plant.
  4. A mandate from a major processor that cannot be confirmed through the group’s official channel.
  5. An upfront fee to “accredit,” “register,” or “release” the cargo.
  6. Thematically incoherent certification - for example, a wrong technical description of the cut, a halal seal where it makes no sense, or vague cold-chain specs.
  7. A 48-hour rush and refusal to provide a POP (Proof of Product) verifiable at the source.
  8. A third-party payment account, in a jurisdiction with no connection to the plant or the goods.

How to verify (practical steps)

  1. Identify the plant of origin. Demand the name, the CNPJ, and the SIF number of the establishment that actually slaughters and ships. Legitimate chicken leaves a concrete plant, not an abstract trading company.
  2. Confirm the GACC accreditation. The list of foreign plants accredited to export to China is public on the official Chinese customs platform (GACC / Single Window - the CIFER system for registering importing manufacturers). Check whether the plant and the registration number cited appear there, and whether the poultry accreditation is active.
  3. Confirm the SIF/MAPA. Verify the establishment’s registration with MAPA’s Servico de Inspecao Federal. Plants accredited to export poultry meat appear in the official records.
  4. Validate the mandate at the source. Call the processor’s institutional phone number and write to its real corporate domain - never to the contacts the intermediary provided - and ask whether that person represents them. A mandate that does not survive a call to the origin is worthless.
  5. Demand verifiable proof of product (POP). An independent inspection confirmed directly with the inspector, a video with the day’s code, batch data that matches the plant. A real seller wants to prove they have the cargo.
  6. Confirm banking details through a second channel and refuse any upfront fee.

Frequently asked questions

How do I confirm whether a Brazilian plant is accredited to export chicken to China?
The list of foreign establishments accredited by the GACC is public on the official Chinese customs platform (Single Window / CIFER system). Check whether the plant name and the registration number cited appear there and whether the poultry accreditation is active. In parallel, confirm the establishment's SIF registration with MAPA. If the seller cannot point to a listed plant, treat it as a NO-GO.
Can a small foreign trading company legitimately sell Brazilian frozen chicken?
In theory intermediaries exist, but chicken bound for China leaves SIF plants accredited by the GACC, which belong to large groups with their own channels. A large volume offered by a micro-trading company with no structure or track record in the poultry sector, at a below-market price, is an incongruence between the structure and the operation. Demand to trace the product back to a listed plant before moving forward.
They asked for a fee to 'accredit the lot with the GACC.' Is that real?
That is a classic pattern of an advance-fee disguised as a regulatory cost. GACC accreditation belongs to the exporting plant, not a lot that the buyer 'registers' by paying an upfront fee. In legitimate exports, regulatory and inspection costs belong to the seller or are already in the price. Any charge to 'release,' 'register,' or 'activate' the cargo before delivery should be treated as a NO-GO.
