Due diligence in soybeans and grains from Brazil to China

Updated 2026-06-19 · 5 min read
Bulk soybeans and corn for China move enormous volumes and attract fake-mandate and payment-diversion scams. See the red flags and how to verify the backing.

Brazil is the world’s largest soybean exporter, and China is its main destination. The operation involves gigantic bulk volumes, Panamax and Capesize vessels, and per-contract values that easily reach tens of millions of dollars. That scale is exactly what attracts fraudsters: in a negotiation of this size, a single diverted advance is already an enormous loss. This article shows what the legitimate operation of bulk soybeans and corn from Brazil to China looks like, the scams typical of the route, the specific red flags, and how to verify the seller and the backing before paying.

Read it alongside our pillar guide to anti-fraud due diligence in commodities: the volumes change, but the choreography of the fraud is the same.

What the legitimate operation looks like

Bulk soybeans and corn are markets of large players: the so-called ABCD (the largest global grain trading houses), large Brazilian cooperatives, crushers, and exporters with a CNPJ, a track record, and their own logistics structure. The real operation has striking features:

  • Volume and logistics matched. A bulk soybean shipment requires access to a port terminal, a shipping window, origination capacity (buying the grain in the interior), and contracted ocean freight. This is not improvised.
  • A clear technical specification - soybeans in grain, oil and protein content, moisture, impurities, damaged grains; or corn with quality and moisture specs.
  • Accreditation and phytosanitary compliance. China requires phytosanitary compliance and the registration of exporters/warehouses with the GACC for grains; the shipment is accompanied by a phytosanitary certificate issued by the Brazilian authority.
  • Coherent Incoterms and documents - FOB or CIF, a BL (Bill of Lading), a certificate of inspection (weight, quality) by an independent third party such as SGS.
  • Banking instruments in the right order - a documentary LC or an SBLC operated bank to bank, against conforming shipping documents.

The key point: a legitimate bulk soybean operation is traceable back to real origination and real logistics. Whoever has no terminal, freight, and origination does not move 50 thousand tonnes.

The scams typical of the soybean/grain route

Fake mandate and representation

The central typology. Someone presents themselves as the mandate or representative of a major Brazilian trading house or cooperative, using the real name, letterhead, and even plant and terminal data. The fake mandate scam borrows the credibility of a known brand to close fast: SCOs appear with below-market prices, an NCNDA is demanded on first contact, and an ICPO is requested to “lock in” the volume. The representation is never confirmed through the named company’s official channel.

Payment diversion

This typology attacks real operations, including between legitimate parties - and that is why it is so dangerous in soybean contracts, where the value per wire is extremely high. A criminal compromises an email (of the supplier, the buyer, or an intermediary) and, close to payment, sends new “updated” banking details: “we changed banks,” “use this branch.” The money goes to the fraudster’s account. It is the classic BEC (Business Email Compromise): the deal is genuine, only the destination of the money has been hijacked.

Broker chains and abusive non-circumvention

Between you and the supposed origination there is a queue of intermediaries, each demanding an NCNDA and invoking non-circumvention clauses to justify why you never speak to the real trading house. In the scam, the clause becomes a curtain: it serves to prevent verification, not to protect a real commission.

Advance-fee and unbacked instruments

Upfront fees appear “to secure the shipping window,” a “performance bond,” or “SBLC leasing” structures. In a legitimate grain operation, guarantees move bank to bank, against documents - not as an upfront fee paid to an intermediary.

Red flags specific to the Brazil->China soybean/grain route

  1. A seller with no demonstrable logistics - promises 50 thousand tonnes but cannot point to a terminal, origination, or freight.
  2. A mandate from a major trading house/cooperative that cannot be confirmed at the official source.
  3. A long chain of brokers hiding the origination “because of the NCNDA.”
  4. A last-minute change of banking details close to payment (payment diversion).
  5. An advance-fee or “SBLC leasing” in place of bank-to-bank instruments.
  6. A price far below the curve + a 48-hour rush.
  7. A third-party payment account or one in a jurisdiction with no connection to the exporter.
  8. Specs or volume incoherent with the named company’s real capacity.

How to verify the seller and the backing

  1. Identify the real counterparty and check the registry. Confirm the CNPJ in Brazil, the track record, and a line of business compatible with exporting bulk grain. A recently formed company, or one with a disconnected business purpose, moving tens of thousands of tonnes is a front pattern.
  2. Validate the mandate at the source. Call the trading house’s/cooperative’s institutional phone number and write to its real corporate domain - never to the intermediary’s contacts - and ask whether that person represents it. A mandate that does not survive a call to the origin is worthless.
  3. Verify the logistical backing. Bulk soybeans require a terminal, a shipping window, and origination. Ask for proof of capacity: a terminal contract, vessel nomination, a verifiable POP. Whoever has the cargo can demonstrate the logistics; whoever only sells paper cannot.
  4. Confirm banking details through a second channel. Before any wire, call a number already known for the supplier (never the one that came in the same email) and confirm the account. A last-minute change = stop everything.
  5. Keep the right order. Real identification -> proof of product/backing -> SPA -> reciprocal KYC -> banking instruments against documents. Any request for money before the proof breaks the choreography on purpose.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know whether a soybean seller really has the backing to deliver the volume offered?
Bulk soybeans require a port terminal, a shipping window, grain origination, and ocean freight. Ask for proof of logistical capacity: a terminal contract or access, vessel nomination, and a verifiable POP. Whoever has the cargo can demonstrate the logistics; whoever promises 50 thousand tonnes without pointing to a terminal, origination, or freight is probably selling paper, not goods.
Why is payment diversion especially dangerous in soybean operations?
Because the values per wire are extremely high, so a single diversion is already an enormous loss. In a BEC, a criminal compromises an email and, close to payment, sends new banking details claiming a change of bank. The deal may be real; only the destination of the money has been hijacked. Always confirm any banking detail through a second independent channel, using a number already known.
An intermediary says I cannot speak to the trading house 'because of the NCNDA.' Should I accept that?
Be suspicious. The non-circumvention clause exists legitimately to protect commissions, but in the scam it becomes a curtain to prevent verification. You have the right to confirm the representation at the named trading house's official source. A long chain of brokers that refuses to reveal the origination, demanding signatures before any proof of product, is a pattern consistent with a fake mandate.

Before you sign, find out who you are dealing with.
