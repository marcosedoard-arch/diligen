ICUMSA 45 sugar fraud: how to protect yourself from the scam

Updated 2026-06-18 · 5 min read
ICUMSA 45 sugar is the product most used in commodity scams. See the typical schemes, the red flags, and how to demand verifiable proof from the counterparty.

Few commodities show up in scams as often as Brazilian sugar, and the reason is simple: the acronym ICUMSA has become a password. Offers of “ICUMSA 45” flood the inboxes of buyers all over the world, with below-market prices, enormous volumes, and immediate availability. Most never ship a single bag. This article explains what the real sugar operation looks like, the scams typical of the ICUMSA niche, the specific red flags, and how to demand verifiable proof before signing or paying.

It is worth reading alongside our pillar guide to anti-fraud due diligence in commodities: the engineering underneath these scams is almost always the same - only the front changes.

What ICUMSA is and what the legitimate operation looks like

ICUMSA (International Commission for Uniform Methods of Sugar Analysis) is the international standard for methods of sugar analysis. In commercial practice, the ICUMSA number indicates the color/refinement of the sugar: the lower the number, the whiter and more refined the product.

  • ICUMSA 45 - refined white sugar, the most sought after for consumption.
  • ICUMSA 100/150 - crystal white.
  • ICUMSA 600 to 1200 - raw sugar (raw/VHP), typical for bulk export and refining at destination.

A real operation has a coherent technical specification (polarization, moisture, ICUMSA color, granulometry), defined packaging (50 kg bags, big bags, bulk), clear Incoterms (FOB, CIF), a port of shipment, a schedule, and inspection by an independent third party. The legitimate seller is a mill, an established trading company, or an exporter with a CNPJ, a track record, and a compatible line of business - traceable back to a concrete origin.

The scams typical of ICUMSA sugar

The “sells-everything” pattern / multi-commodity menu

One of the most revealing signs: the same “seller” offers ICUMSA 45 sugar, soybean oil, urea, gold, Jet A1 petroleum, and cement - all on the same list, all at opportunity prices. No real operator masters ICUMSA, EN590, and gold at the same time with immediate stock of each. The multi-commodity menu is the signature of someone who has none of the products: they sell paper, not goods.

Advance-fee with SBLC/leasing

Here the “product” stops being the sugar and becomes the financial instrument. The deal advances to the point where you must pay an upfront fee - or, worse, the scheme revolves around “SBLC leasing,” “BG/SBLC monetization,” “MT760 activation.” The very phrase “SBLC leasing” is already an alert: serious banks do not lease unbacked guarantees to third parties. The advance-fee structure stacks charges - “registration fee,” “performance bond,” “advance inspection cost” - each one “the last.”

Refusal of POP/SGS

When you ask for proof, the fraudster stalls. You ask for a verifiable POP (Proof of Product) or an SGS inspection confirmed at the source, and the answer is evasive: “that only after the NCNDA and the deposit,” “the POP comes after the ICPO,” more paper documents instead of checkable proof. A real seller wants to prove they have the sugar; a fraudster wants to delay the proof until you pay.

Fake mandate and forged documents

As on the entire Brazilian commodities route, the fake mandate scam appears: someone claims to represent a major mill, with an SCO, a proforma, a “quality certificate,” and warehouse photos - material that serves to create the sense of a real operation without one existing.

Red flags specific to ICUMSA sugar

  1. A multi-commodity menu - sugar listed alongside oil, urea, gold, fuel.
  2. A technically incorrect ICUMSA description - conflating the number with purity incorrectly, vague or incoherent specs (a color that does not match the stated polarization).
  3. SBLC leasing/monetization or a request for a fee to “issue the MT760.”
  4. An advance-fee under any regulatory name.
  5. Refusal or postponement of POP/SGS until payment.
  6. A mill mandate that cannot be confirmed at the official source.
  7. A 48-hour rush and a price far below the curve.
  8. A third-party payment account, outside the exporter’s name.

How to demand verifiable proof and validate the counterparty

  1. Identify the real counterparty (not just the broker) and confirm it exists in the public registry - CNPJ in Brazil - with a track record and a line of business compatible with exporting sugar. A “consultancy” offering 25 thousand tonnes is a front pattern.
  2. Demand a POP verifiable at the source. Do not accept a PDF the seller sends you. Ask for an inspection by an independent third party (SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek) confirmed directly with the inspector, and a POP whose numbers can be validated.
  3. Check the technical coherence. Real ICUMSA 45 specs carry polarization, moisture, ICUMSA color, and granulometry coherent with one another. A technical error from someone who should master the product is a sign that the counterparty does not actually operate in the sugar market.
  4. Refuse the inversion of the order. In a sound operation, the seller proves the product before you expose yourself. Were you asked for a fee, an SBLC, or a deposit before the proof? The order was broken on purpose.
  5. Confirm banking details through a second channel and never pay an upfront fee to “release” anything.

Frequently asked questions

What does the ICUMSA number mean and why is it cited so often in scams?
ICUMSA is the international standard for methods of sugar analysis; the number indicates the color/refinement, where the lower it is, the whiter the sugar (ICUMSA 45 is the most sought-after refined white). The acronym became a scam password because it sounds technical and creates instant credibility. Offers of 'ICUMSA 45' at below-market prices, with enormous volume and immediate availability, are the classic bait.
The seller offers sugar, soybean oil, urea, and gold on the same list. Is that normal?
No. The multi-commodity menu is one of the clearest signatures of fraud. No real operator masters ICUMSA, fuel, and gold at the same time with immediate stock of each. Whoever offers everything usually has nothing: they sell paper, not goods. Treat it as a strong red flag and demand to trace the concrete origin of the sugar.
How do I ask for verifiable proof that the sugar actually exists?
Demand a POP (Proof of Product) whose numbers can be validated and an inspection by an independent third party (SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek) confirmed directly with the inspector, not a PDF the seller sends you. Refuse the inversion of the order: proof of product comes before any fee, SBLC, or deposit. A seller who delays the proof until payment is a scam pattern.
