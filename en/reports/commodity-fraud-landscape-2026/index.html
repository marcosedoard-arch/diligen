The State of Fraud in Commodities and Trade — 2026

Updated 2026-06-19 · 8 min read
Public, current data (FBI IC3, GAFI/FATF, ICC IMB, GFI, UNODC, INTERPOL) on BEC, advance-fee, and TBML in commodities trade along the Brazil-world axis.

Every year, billions of dollars cross borders in commodities contracts — soybeans, sugar, coffee, animal protein, ore, fuels. And every year, a fraction of those flows is diverted, laundered, or simply never existed at all, hidden behind well-written emails, plausible documents, and counterparties that look real. This report brings together public, current data from the field’s leading authorities to answer a practical question: what is, today, the size and shape of fraud risk for those who buy and sell commodities along the Brazil-world axis?

The methodological premise is simple and non-negotiable: every number here has a named source and a year. Where no reliable estimate exists, we prefer to describe the phenomenon qualitatively rather than invent a figure.

The scale of the problem

The sharpest picture of financial crime enabled by social engineering comes from the United States. In 2024, the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center recorded 859,532 complaints and reported losses of US$ 16.6 billion — a 33% increase over 2023 (Source: FBI IC3 Internet Crime Report 2024). This is not the entire universe of fraud: it is only what was reported, by victims mostly in the US, to a single channel. Even so, it serves as a trend thermometer, and the trend is upward.

Within that total, Business Email Compromise (BEC) — the category that covers the hijacking or spoofing of corporate emails to divert payments — accounted for roughly US$ 2.77 billion in losses, spread across 21,442 complaints in 2024 (Source: FBI IC3 Internet Crime Report 2024). In number of occurrences, BEC does not even rank among the most frequent, but in value per scam it sits at the top: few incidents, high losses. Looking at the historical series, the FBI estimates that BEC caused approximately US$ 55 billion in global losses between 2013 and 2023 (Source: FBI IC3 Public Service Announcement, Sept/2024).

When money needs to be moved or justified, international trade becomes the vehicle. Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML) uses commercial transactions that appear legitimate — through over- or under-invoicing, false description of goods, multiple invoicing — to mask the origin of the funds. The GAFI/FATF, together with the Egmont Group, classifies TBML as one of the most complex and hardest-to-detect laundering techniques, precisely because it hides within the normal trade flow; the authority itself acknowledges the difficulty of precisely measuring the global scale of the phenomenon (Source: FATF/Egmont, Trade-Based Money Laundering: Trends and Developments, 2020).

An indirect indicator of the magnitude comes from Global Financial Integrity, which measures the “gap” between what countries declare they export and what their partners declare they import. GFI identified roughly US$ 1.6 trillion in potential trade misinvoicing across 134 developing countries in 2018 alone (Source: Global Financial Integrity, Dec/2021). Not every gap is a crime — differences in classification and timing exist — but the order of magnitude shows how much room trade offers for manipulation. As a macro reference, the UNODC estimates that between 2% and 5% of global GDP is laundered each year (Source: UNODC).

In the physical link of the chain, risk also persists. The ICC’s International Maritime Bureau recorded 116 incidents of piracy and armed robbery against ships in 2024, with 94 vessels boarded and 6 hijacked; worryingly, 126 crew members were taken hostage during the year, against 73 in 2023 (Source: ICC IMB Piracy and Armed Robbery Report 2024). Maritime risk is not only about cargo: it is about people, routes, and insurance premiums.

The most active typologies

The false mandate is the classic point of entry in commodities trade. A supposed representative of a large producer or refinery offers attractive volume and price; they present a “mandate,” LOI, template contracts, and copies of documents. The goods do not exist, or the intermediary has no relationship whatsoever with the producer they claim to represent. The scam thrives on haste, exclusivity, and the counterparty’s difficulty in verifying the chain back to the source.

The advance-fee is usually the monetary climax of the false mandate: before shipment, a series of upfront payments is requested — “performance bond,” inspection fee, customs cost, document release. Each step generates a new fee, and the delivery never arrives. It is the oldest typology of the genre and remains effective because it anchors itself in documents that look official.

The payment diversion is BEC applied to trade: after a legitimate negotiation, the fraudster intercepts the communication and, at the moment of payment, sends altered banking instructions — generally claiming a “change of bank” or “account under maintenance.” Here the number speaks: it was the ~US$ 2.77 billion in BEC losses reported in 2024 (Source: FBI IC3 Internet Crime Report 2024), and the victim often only finds out when the real supplier demands a payment that has already gone out to another account.

TBML is less a one-off scam and more a structural method: over-/under-invoicing, false description of goods, and multiple invoicing make it possible to move value between jurisdictions under a commercial appearance. The GAFI/FATF points to it as one of the most-used and hardest-to-detect techniques (Source: FATF/Egmont, 2020).

Fake financial instruments — forged SBLC (Standby Letter of Credit), BG (Bank Guarantee), and POF — appear to lend a veneer of credibility to a nonexistent deal or to extract fees. Cloned documents, shell banks, and “verification” platforms controlled by the fraudster themselves are recurring features. In parallel, quality/origin fraud — adulterating, relabeling, or falsifying product designation — has concrete expression: Operation OPSON XIII, conducted by INTERPOL and Europol, seized roughly € 91 million in fraudulent food and beverages in 2024, with 22 thousand tonnes pulled from the market (Source: INTERPOL/Europol, Operation OPSON XIII, 2024).

The Brazil / commodities angle

Brazil is one of the world’s largest agricultural exporters, which places it at the center of the exposure. The greater the legitimate flow of soybeans, sugar, coffee, and animal protein, the more attractive the environment becomes for anyone wanting to pass themselves off as a Brazilian producer, trading house, or refinery — and the more noise there is for a scam to camouflage itself.

Three factors amplify the risk along the Brazil-world axis. First, the distance and information asymmetry: the buyer abroad rarely knows the plant, the port, or the cooperative of origin, and depends on documents that are easy to forge. Second, the chain of intermediaries: brokers of brokers, “facilitators,” and serial mandates dilute responsibility and conceal the real counterparty. Third, the price and deadline pressure: “below-market” offers, with a short window and a demand for exclusivity, are exactly the terrain where advance-fee flourishes.

These vectors are not exclusive to Brazil, but they intensify in high-volume commodities of geographically concentrated origin — precisely the profile of Brazil’s main export lines. The maritime risk to cargo, in turn, is global and systemic (Source: ICC IMB 2024), reinforcing that diligence does not end at signing: it continues at shipment, in the document, and in the destination account.

What this means for those who buy/sell

The central lesson of the data is that most of the loss does not come from sophisticated hackers, but from poorly calibrated trust: an email, a document, and a bank account that no one verified independently. Three moves drastically reduce exposure.

  1. Verify the counterparty at the source, not on paper. Confirm legal existence, ownership, track record, and the real relationship between who is offering and who is producing — before any payment. A mandate and an LOI are not proof of anything on their own.
  2. Treat a change of banking details as a critical event. Any change to payment instructions must be confirmed through an independent, previously agreed channel, never through the same email that requested it. This is the direct defense against payment diversion and BEC (Source: FBI IC3 2024).
  3. Be suspicious of haste, exclusivity, and upfront fees. The combo “unbeatable price + urgency + advance payment” is the signature of advance-fee. No advance fee should be paid before validating the underlying asset and the counterparty.

The practical decision comes down to GO, NO-GO, or EDD (enhanced due diligence): proceed, decline, or dig deeper. The cost of a verification is a fraction of the value at risk in a single diverted contract.

Methodological note and sources

This report is an original synthesis of public data. There are no proprietary numbers or internal estimates: every figure cited refers to a recognized authority and a specific year. Where the official literature acknowledges that no reliable estimate exists — as with the global scale of TBML — we opted for a qualitative description. The language is indicative and does not refer to any specific real entity.
