SBLC: what it is and the standby letter scam

Updated 2026-06-19 · 2 min read
SBLC (Standby Letter of Credit) is the standby credit instrument used as a guarantee.

SBLC (Standby Letter of Credit) is a standby credit instrument: a guarantee issued by a bank that commits to pay the beneficiary if the contracting party fails to meet its obligation, working as a safety net triggered only in the event of default.

What it is

Unlike an ordinary commercial Letter of Credit, which is the intended means of payment, the SBLC is a backstop guarantee: ideally it is never drawn, because it is only triggered if something goes wrong. It is issued by a bank in favor of a beneficiary, subject to recognized international rules, and works as a bank promise of payment conditioned on default.

How it is used in commodities trading

In commodities trading, the SBLC has a legitimate use as a performance or payment guarantee, giving comfort to one of the parties against the risk of default by the other. It appears in structured deals, generally after mutual verification and a clear definition of contractual terms.

Relevance to fraud and due diligence

The SBLC is surrounded by two recurring scams. The first is SBLC “leasing” or “monetization”: the lease of a standby letter is offered, or a promise is made to convert it into cash, in exchange for advance fees; the promised instrument generally does not exist, is unbacked, or is never delivered, and the fraudster’s gain is the fees. The second pattern arises within the commodities negotiation itself: the supposed seller demands that the buyer issue an SBLC, or make a guaranteed payment, before presenting any verifiable proof that the cargo exists. This reversal is a strong red flag. The safe sequence puts proof of product and counterparty verification before the buyer commits bank guarantees. Terms such as “MTN” instruments, “bank guarantee” for lease, and promises of quick returns on standby letters tend to accompany these offers. In a due diligence review, a demand for an SBLC from the buyer before proof of cargo, or invitations to monetize letters, weigh heavily on the recommendation.

Frequently asked questions

What is a legitimate SBLC for?
As a backstop guarantee: the bank pays the beneficiary if the counterparty fails to meet its obligation. Ideally it is never drawn, since it works as a safety net against default, not as a current means of payment.
What is the SBLC monetization or leasing scam?
A fraud in which someone offers to lease an SBLC or convert it into cash in exchange for advance fees. The promised instrument usually does not exist or is never delivered; the fraudster's goal is the fees paid up front.
Should I issue an SBLC before the seller proves the cargo?
Demanding a guarantee from the buyer before any verifiable proof of product inverts the safe order and is a warning sign. Counterparty verification and proof of cargo should precede any commitment of guarantees.
