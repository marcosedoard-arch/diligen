Advance-fee fraud: what it is

Updated 2026-06-20 · 2 min read
Advance-fee fraud is the scam that demands an upfront payment, such as fees, activation, or a good-faith deposit, before any verifiable consideration.

Advance-fee fraud is the scam in which the victim is led to pay amounts up front, presented as fees, activation costs, a good-faith deposit, or administrative expenses, in exchange for a larger consideration that never materializes in any verifiable way.

What it is

The logic of the scam is simple: promise something of great value, a discounted commodity cargo, the release of a financial instrument, a hefty commission, and demand, as a precondition, a relatively small payment. That payment is framed to look routine: a transfer fee, documentation cost, “performance bond,” good-faith deposit, activation or storage fee. The promised consideration is disproportionate to the amount requested, which makes the advance seem reasonable to the victim.

How it is used in commodities trading

In commodities trading, advance-fee fraud attaches itself to the other instruments. After the exchange of SCO and ICPO, and backed by an unconfirmed mandate letter, the fraudster introduces a fee that must be paid “to release the POP,” “to register the contract,” “to activate the SBLC,” or “to secure the lot.” The request always precedes any verifiable proof of product or legitimacy, and is justified by urgency and short deadlines.

Relevance to fraud and due diligence

The hallmark is the escalation: once the first amount is paid, a new demand appears, an unexpected fee, a tax, a penalty, always presented as the last obstacle before delivery. Each payment increases the victim’s commitment and is used to extract the next one, until contact simply stops, the classic ending in silence. The protective principle is direct: no payment should precede a verifiable consideration, and proof of product and counterparty verification always come before money. In a due diligence review, any demand for upfront payment before confirmable proof is treated as a strong red flag and usually supports a NO-GO verdict.

Frequently asked questions

What is the clearest sign of advance-fee fraud?
The demand for any payment, fee, good-faith deposit, or activation charge, before any verifiable consideration. In a legitimate deal, proof of product and counterparty verification precede the disbursement of money.
Why do the fees tend to grow over time?
Because the escalation is part of the scam. Each payment is used to justify the next, always described as the final obstacle, until the victim is exhausted or the fraudster disappears in silence.
Is a good-faith deposit a legitimate request?
It can be used legitimately in some contexts, but in commodities trading, when demanded before verifiable proof of product and confirmation of the counterparty, it is a classic red flag of advance-fee fraud.
