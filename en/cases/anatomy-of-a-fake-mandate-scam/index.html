Anatomy of a false-mandate scam: chicken Brazil→China

Updated 2026-06-29 · 10 min read
How we dismantled, in just a few days, a frozen-chicken offer Brazil→China built on a false mandate, advance-fee mechanics and pressure for an upfront payment. Verdict: NO-GO.

When the “opportunity of the quarter” shows up in a hurry

Almost every big fraud begins with good news. In the case we are going to reconstruct here (anonymized, but real down to the last structural detail), the good news was a firm offer of frozen chicken “from Brazil” to China: a competitive price, meaningful volume and the promise of recurring shipments. The recipient, from here on our client, is an experienced international buyer. Even so, he paused and brought us in before moving any money. That was the decision that kept this story out of the crime pages.

The offer arrived the way it almost always arrives in this market: through a chain of brokers. One intermediary introduced the next, who introduced the next, and somewhere along that chain the heavyweight name appeared: a mandate supposedly issued by one of the largest Brazilian exporters of animal protein. The documentation package looked apparently complete: a Soft Corporate Offer (SCO) with prices and terms, an NCNDA circulating to “protect the commissions” of the chain, a request for an ICPO from the buyer and, at the center of it all, a mandate letter in PDF — logo, signature, corporate language — “authorizing” an intermediary to negotiate on behalf of the large exporter.

There was just one detail that kept us up at night: the hurry. The price window “closed in hours.” A good-faith deposit was requested. There was talk of an upfront payment to “lock in the production allocation.” And the engineering of the operation required part of the money to leave before any concrete proof that the cargo, the producer and the counterparty actually existed.

To size up what was at stake: in this market, a 40’ container of frozen chicken runs in the range of USD 80–130k. A “test order” of a few containers with a 30% upfront payment already exceeds USD 100k leaving the buyer’s account, going to a counterparty he had never met in person, validated only by documents sent over email. Before committing a single cent, the client engaged a Counterparty X-Ray.

The method: look in layers, before the contract is yours

We don’t hand over the gold: part of our value is precisely how we cross-reference the sources. But the logic of the work is public, because rigor isn’t a secret, it’s discipline. When they brought us in, we opened up the counterparty and the documents presented in layers that talk to each other. None of them, on its own, convicts or absolves; it’s the overlap that produces a reading.

The first is the documentary and forensic layer. Does the document say what it claims to be? We look beyond the text: file structure, metadata, authorship, creation tool, dates, coherence between what the paper asserts and how the paper was actually manufactured. A PDF carries far more than the eye reads.

The second is the multi-jurisdiction registry layer. Do the cited entities — the intermediary, his supposed company, the “trading,” the mandating producer — exist in the official records of the countries where they claim to exist? And does what they claim to do match what they are registered to do? This is where our wedge comes in: access to the Brazilian registry (registration status, address, economic activity of the cited producer) which a foreign counterparty, on its own, simply cannot reach.

The third is the OSINT layer: digital footprint, history, public presence, consistency between what the counterparty claims and what the open world shows (or fails to show). The fourth is the financial and payment-structure layer: how the money is requested, and whom the proposed mechanics protect, the one who pays, or only the one who receives? The fifth is the fraud-typology layer: what does this arrangement look like? We cross-reference the findings against known patterns of trade finance fraud — false mandate, broker chain, advance-fee.

Each finding enters the report with a confidence level (High, Medium or Low) and an evidence attachment: a dated screenshot, the file hash, the source. We don’t ask the client to take our word for it. We show it.

The findings: piece by piece, the mandate collapsed

Taken in isolation, some of the points would have an explanation. Together, they formed a picture hard to explain any other way. All the findings below were recorded in cautionary language: indications, not a criminal verdict against identifiable people.

The entity that wasn’t where it should have been

The intermediary presented himself through a limited liability company supposedly incorporated in the United States. A search of the official corporate registry of the claimed state did not locate the entity under the terms provided. Confidence: High. A company that, in theory, moves six figures per shipment but does not appear where it should is a first-order indication. It’s not a bureaucratic detail. It’s the nonexistent foundation beneath the entire building.

The envelope contradicted the letter

The mandate letter — the document that gave the whole operation its weight — carried, in its internal metadata, markers incompatible with the legal department of a large exporter. The file had been generated with a non-corporate editing tool, and a generic personal email was recorded as the document’s owner/author. Confidence: High. Mandates from companies of that size are not born like this, in a homemade editor signed off by a personal account. The content said “large exporter”; the technical envelope said something else.

The corporate costume didn’t match the role

One of the legs of the structure was a recently incorporated trading company, registered outside Brazil, presented as a global commodities operator. On checking its registered purpose and economic activity, we found something incompatible with international protein trading at volume. Confidence: Medium–High. A meat-export operation at scale leaves a trail: structure, history, a coherent declared activity. What we had before us was a recent shell dressed up as a giant.

The heavyweight name used as bait

When we checked, in the Brazilian registry, the producer that supposedly issued the mandate, we found divergences between the registration record and the address/identification actually associated with that reference. Confidence: Medium. The heavyweight name was being invoked, but the data around it didn’t tie together. It’s a pattern consistent with reputation hijacking: you take a well-known, credible brand and stick to it an operation it knows nothing about.

Whoever has the cargo shows the cargo

We asked for the basics that a real operation delivers without drama: evidence of shipment capacity, proof of prior operations, primary confirmation from the very exporter supposedly mandating the deal. The response was evasive, conditioned on the upfront payment, or simply never came. Confidence: High as a behavioral signal. The systematic refusal to provide proof of performance, conditioned on money paid in advance, is not negotiating shyness. It’s a data point.

The certification that didn’t fit the destination

Elements of sanitary certification and accreditation mentioned in the offer were incongruent with the requirements of the declared destination. Confidence: Medium. Exporting protein to a market like China goes through specific accreditation requirements. The piece presented didn’t fit the board: one more item that didn’t slot into the puzzle.

The payment structure that protected only one side

The financial design pushed the risk entirely onto the buyer: money paid in advance, upfront, “to release” stages, with no instrument to protect the one paying, and before any independent verification. Confidence: High. It’s the classic signature of the advance-fee scam: the mechanics are designed so the money goes out first and the questions come later — when there’s no longer anyone to ask.

The thesis: it wasn’t seven coincidences, it was a pattern

In isolation, they’re loose pieces. Overlaid, they form a coherent picture. The combination — a phantom entity, a document probably forged in a homemade tool, an incompatible corporate costume, the misuse of a large producer’s name, the refusal of proof and the pressure for an upfront payment — is consistent with a reusable fraud “kit”: the same false-mandate engineering, dressed up as a legitimate operation, assembled to extract an advance payment and disappear. Swap out the commodity, the route and the names; the skeleton stays the same.

We do not assert a crime by any identifiable person or company; it was not possible to confirm any of that, and that is not our role. We assert something more useful for the client’s decision: the body of indications did not support proceeding.

The verdict and the outcome

Within the deadline, we delivered a Counterparty X-Ray with a risk classification and a clear recommendation:

NO-GO. Do not proceed with the operation as presented. Should the client wish to insist, only under EDD — enhanced due diligence — and subject to conditions that the counterparty had, in practice, already been refusing to meet.

The client did not send the upfront payment.

The “can’t-miss” opportunity evaporated, the way offers evaporate when they depend on you paying before you can verify. What did not evaporate was the client’s capital, the cargo that would never exist and his name, which was not attached to a fraudulent operation. Given the order of magnitude of what was on the table, the report cost a fraction of what was about to leave the account. Price isn’t everything. But in this decision, it was 100%.

What this case teaches

  • The hurry is part of the scam, not a detail of the negotiation. “The window closes in hours” exists so you don’t have time to verify. A real operation survives 48–72h of checking.
  • A pretty document is not a true document. A logo and a signature convince the eye; metadata, registries and structure tell the truth.
  • The big producer’s name is the favorite bait. A mandate from a heavyweight exporter gives instant credibility, and that’s exactly why it’s the thing most often forged.
  • Whoever has the cargo shows the cargo. Refusing proof of performance while conditioning it on an upfront payment is, in itself, a signal.
  • The upfront payment before verification is the point of no return. Once the money is out, your leverage is gone. Verify before the contract — and the risk — is yours.

Frequently asked questions

What is a false-mandate fraud in commodities trading?
It's a scam in which someone pretends to hold authorization (a "mandate") to sell on behalf of a large, well-known producer. The offer arrives via a broker chain, accompanied by a mandate letter, an SCO, an NCNDA and a request for an ICPO, all professional in appearance. The goal isn't to deliver the cargo, but to use the heavyweight name to extract an upfront payment. It's a pattern consistent with advance-fee: the money goes out before any proof that the operation exists.
How can you verify whether a mandate letter is authentic?
The text and the logo aren't enough: they convince the eye, not the analysis. We examine the PDF metadata (creation tool, author, dates), cross-check the issuing entity against the official registries of the jurisdiction, check the registered economic activity and seek primary confirmation from the producer supposedly mandating the deal. Indications such as a generic personal email as the file's author, or a non-corporate editing tool, are relevant red flags.
Why is pressure for a quick upfront payment a red flag?
Because the hurry tends to be part of the scam, not part of the negotiation. "The window closes in hours" exists so you don't have time to verify. A legitimate operation survives 48–72h of checking and provides proof of performance without conditioning it on payment. When the structure demands money in advance, with no instrument to protect the one paying and before any independent verification, the risk lies entirely on the buyer's side.
